In life-threatening situations — call emergency services (911 / 123) immediately before any action. This guide does not replace professional medical care.

The Golden Rule

The First 5 Minutes Matter Most

Understanding what to prioritise in any emergency can be the difference between life and lasting harm

1
0–30 sec

Ensure Scene Safety

Never rush in blindly. Check for fire, traffic, live wires, or toxic fumes. A rescuer who becomes a victim helps no one. Assess before you act.

2
30–60 sec

Call for Help — 911 / 123

Get professional help on the way immediately. Even while you help, emergency dispatch can guide you in real time. Note the time the incident started.

3
1–3 min

Address Life Threats First

Prioritise: unresponsiveness → airway → breathing → severe bleeding. These three conditions kill fastest. Use the guides below for each situation.

4
3–5 min

Monitor & Reassure

Once immediate threats are managed, keep the person calm and still. Continuously monitor breathing and consciousness until paramedics arrive.

5
Handover

Brief the Paramedics

Tell them: what happened, when it happened, what you've done, any medications the person takes, and any changes in their condition.

Fast Facts

Did You Know?

Science-backed facts every person should know before an emergency happens.

CPR

Bystander CPR Doubles Survival

Immediate bystander CPR can double or even triple a cardiac arrest victim's chance of survival before paramedics arrive.

Stroke

1.9 Million Neurons Lost Per Minute

During a stroke, approximately 1.9 million brain cells die every minute. Getting to hospital fast is the single most important action.

Burns

Cool Water Works Up to 3 Hours Later

Running cool water over a burn for 20 minutes reduces injury depth even when started up to 3 hours after the burn occurred.

Bleeding

Direct Pressure is the Gold Standard

Continuous direct pressure on a wound is the most effective method to stop bleeding — more effective than elevation alone.

Choking

Back Blows First, Always

Current guidelines recommend starting with back blows before abdominal thrusts — they're often sufficient to clear the obstruction.

Preparedness

Only 18% of Homes Have a Full Kit

Studies show less than 1 in 5 households maintain a stocked, up-to-date first aid kit — yet emergencies can happen anywhere.

Essential Skill

The Recovery Position

If someone is unconscious but breathing normally, the recovery position keeps their airway open and prevents choking.

1

Kneel Beside the Person

Position yourself on one side of the person lying on their back. Straighten their legs.

2

Position the Near Arm

Place the arm closest to you at a right angle to the body, elbow bent, palm facing up.

3

Bring the Far Arm Across

Bring their far arm across the chest and hold the back of their hand against their near cheek.

4

Pull the Far Knee Up

With your other hand, pull the far knee up until the foot is flat on the ground.

5

Roll Gently Toward You

Keeping their hand against their cheek, pull on the bent knee and roll them onto their side.

6

Open and Monitor

Tilt their head back gently to open the airway. Monitor breathing until help arrives.

When to Use It

The recovery position is for people who are unconscious but still breathing. It prevents the tongue from blocking the airway and allows fluids to drain.

Unconscious but breathing normally
No suspected spinal or neck injury
Not awake enough to protect their own airway
Do NOT use if spinal injury is possible
Do NOT use if not breathing — start CPR instead
Decision Guide

When to Call for Help

Not every situation needs an ambulance, but some absolutely do. Know the difference.

Call 911 Immediately

  • Unconscious or unresponsive
  • Not breathing or gasping
  • Chest pain or tightness
  • Signs of stroke (FAST)
  • Severe uncontrolled bleeding
  • Suspected overdose or poisoning
  • Severe allergic reaction

Go to A&E Soon

  • Burns larger than your palm
  • Suspected broken bone
  • Wounds needing stitches
  • Head injury with confusion
  • Sudden severe headache
  • Swallowed something unknown

Home Care is Fine

  • Minor cuts and scrapes
  • Small burns (fingertip-size)
  • Bruising without deformity
  • Mild allergic reaction (rash only)
  • Sprains with full movement
  • Nosebleeds stopping within 20 min

CPR — Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation

For unconscious adults who are not breathing normally

Life-threatening emergency
CPR Compression Rate Trainer 100–120 per min
Press Start to practise the correct rhythm
Watch Practical Demonstration
Arabic

Ensure Scene Safety

Quickly assess the environment before approaching. Do not put yourself at risk from traffic, electricity, or other hazards.

Check for Response

Tap both shoulders firmly and shout "Are you OK?" Wait for any reaction. Look, listen, and feel for normal breathing — no more than 10 seconds.

Call 911 / 123 Immediately

If unresponsive, shout for help and ask someone to call while you start CPR. If alone, call quickly then begin compressions.

Open the Airway

Gently tilt the head back and lift the chin. Briefly look and listen for breathing — no more than 10 seconds.

Begin Chest Compressions

Place the heel of your hand on the centre of the chest. Push hard and fast: 5–6 cm deep, 100–120 compressions per minute. Let the chest fully recoil between each compression — don't lean on them.

Rescue Breaths (If Trained)

After 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths — each lasting 1 second, making the chest visibly rise. If untrained, hands-only CPR is still highly effective.

Continue Until Help Arrives

Maintain the 30:2 ratio without pause. Switch with another rescuer every 2 minutes if possible. Stop only when paramedics take over or the person clearly begins to breathe.

Rhythm tip: If untrained, hands-only CPR (compressions only, no breaths) is still very effective. Push to the beat of "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees — it's exactly 100 BPM. Your compressions should be as deep as pressing 2 inches into a sofa cushion.

Choking — Heimlich Maneuver

For adults and children over 1 year with a blocked airway

Life-threatening emergency
Watch Practical Demonstration
Arabic

Identify Severe Choking

The person cannot speak, cough, or breathe. They may clutch their throat — the universal choking sign. A high-pitched sound or silence during attempted breathing means severe blockage.

Get Help Immediately

Shout for someone to call 911/123. Don't leave the person alone. If alone and the maneuver fails, call before attempting further steps.

5 Back Blows

Lean them forward and give 5 firm blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand. Each blow should be a separate, forceful strike.

5 Abdominal Thrusts (Heimlich)

Stand behind them. Make a fist just above the navel, cover with your other hand, and thrust sharply inward and upward 5 times. Use enough force to feel it.

Alternate Until Cleared

Keep alternating 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts until the object is expelled, the person can breathe, or they lose consciousness (then begin CPR).

Infants under 1 year: Never use abdominal thrusts. Use 5 back blows followed by 5 chest thrusts (2 fingers, centre of chest). Hold face-down on your forearm. Always see a doctor after any choking incident in a baby.

Severe Bleeding — Wound Control

For cuts, puncture wounds, and lacerations with significant blood loss

Life-threatening emergency
Watch Practical Demonstration
Arabic

Protect Yourself First

Use gloves if available. If not, use a clean plastic bag, or ask the casualty to apply pressure themselves. Avoid direct contact with blood when possible.

Call 911 for Major Bleeding

Uncontrolled bleeding from a large wound, an amputated limb, or a stab/gunshot wound requires emergency services immediately.

Apply Direct Pressure

Press a clean cloth or dressing firmly onto the wound. Do not remove it if it becomes soaked — apply more material on top. Maintain constant, firm pressure.

Elevate the Injured Limb

If the wound is on an arm or leg and there is no suspected fracture, raise it above heart level to slow bleeding.

Maintain Pressure for 10–15 Minutes

Do not lift the cloth to check — this disturbs clotting. Keep constant pressure for at least 10–15 minutes without peeking.

Bandage Firmly

Once bleeding slows, secure the dressing firmly without cutting circulation. Check for a pulse below the bandage after applying.

Tourniquet (for limb bleeds only): For life-threatening, uncontrolled limb bleeding, apply a tourniquet 5–7 cm above the wound. Write the time of application on the person's skin or on the tourniquet itself and inform paramedics immediately. Never remove it yourself.

Burns — Thermal, Chemical & Electrical

Correct immediate care reduces burn depth and prevents permanent scarring

Watch Practical Demonstration
Arabic

Stop the Source Immediately

Remove the person from heat, extinguish flames (stop, drop, roll), or remove chemically contaminated clothing — unless it's stuck to the skin.

Cool With Running Water — 20 Minutes

Use lukewarm water (not cold, not iced). Cool the burn for a full 20 minutes. Start within 3 hours of injury for best results. Cooling is the single most effective intervention.

Remove Jewellery Gently

Remove rings, watches, and bracelets before swelling begins — but only if not attached to the burn.

Cover Loosely

Cover with cling film laid flat (not wrapped), a clean non-fluffy cloth, or a sterile burn dressing. Do not wrap tightly. Never use cotton wool directly on a burn.

Seek Medical Care

Go to A&E for: burns larger than your palm, burns on the face/hands/genitals/joints, all electrical or chemical burns, and any third-degree burn regardless of size.

Never apply: butter, toothpaste, oil, ice directly, or any cream or home remedy. These trap heat and dramatically increase infection risk. Never burst blisters — they are the body's natural sterile dressing protecting the wound beneath.

Fractures — Bone Immobilisation

Stabilisation and keeping calm are your two most important tools

Watch Practical Demonstration
Arabic

Do Not Move the Person

Especially if the fracture may be in the spine, neck, or pelvis. Movement can cause paralysis. Tell them to stay still.

Call 911 for Serious Fractures

Open fractures (bone visible), suspected spinal injury, significant deformity, and loss of sensation below the injury all require emergency services.

Control Any Bleeding

For open fractures, press gently around (not over) the bone with a clean cloth. Never push a protruding bone back in.

Immobilise in Position Found

Support the limb in whatever position you find it. An improvised splint can be made from a rolled magazine, umbrella, or straight stick with padding between it and the skin.

Check Circulation Every 10 Minutes

Check for pulse, warmth, colour, and sensation in the limb beyond the fracture. Pallor, coldness, or numbness means impaired blood flow — loosen the splint.

Improvised slings & splints: For an arm fracture, a sling can be made from a scarf or triangular bandage. For a leg fracture, you can splint it to the uninjured leg with padding between them and soft ties at the knee and ankle — never over the fracture site.

Stroke — The FAST Rule

Every minute of delay = 2 million brain cells lost — act immediately

Life-threatening emergency
Watch Practical Demonstration
Arabic
F

Face — Ask them to smile

Is one side of their face drooping or numb? An uneven smile is a strong warning sign. Ask: "Can you show me your teeth?"

A

Arms — Raise both together

Does one arm drift downward or feel weak? Ask them to hold both arms out in front and close their eyes. Any drift in one arm is significant.

S

Speech — Repeat a simple sentence

Is their speech slurred, garbled, or are they unable to speak at all? Ask: "The sky is blue" — can they repeat it clearly?

T

Time — Call 911 NOW

If any of the above are present — even if they improve — call 911 immediately. Note the exact time symptoms started. This is vital information for treatment.

While Waiting for the Ambulance

Keep them calm and seated or lying down. Give nothing by mouth. If unconscious and breathing, place in the recovery position (on their side).

The 4.5-hour window: Clot-busting drugs (thrombolytics) only work within 4.5 hours of symptom onset. Thrombectomy may be possible up to 24 hours. Getting to hospital fast is the single biggest factor in stroke outcome — every minute matters enormously.

Anaphylaxis — Severe Allergic Reaction

A whole-body allergic emergency that can be fatal within minutes without treatment

Life-threatening emergency
Watch Practical Demonstration
English

Recognise Anaphylaxis

Any combination of: sudden hives/rash, throat tightening or difficulty swallowing, wheezing or difficulty breathing, swelling of face/lips/tongue, rapid pulse, pale or blotchy skin, dizziness or collapse — especially after known allergen exposure.

Call 911 Immediately

Anaphylaxis is always an emergency. Call even if the person has an EpiPen — they still need hospital monitoring. Say "anaphylaxis" clearly to the dispatcher.

Use the EpiPen (Adrenaline Auto-Injector)

If the person has one — or you have access to one — inject into the outer thigh (through clothing if necessary). Hold in place for 10 seconds, then massage the site. Note the time.

Position Correctly

If breathing is difficult, let them sit up. If they feel faint or have collapsed, lay them down and raise their legs — unless this makes breathing harder. Do not ask them to stand up suddenly.

Prepare for a Second Dose

If there is no improvement after 5 minutes, a second EpiPen can be used if available. The reaction can return (biphasic reaction) even after the person improves — hospital observation is always required.

If Unconscious and Not Breathing

Begin CPR immediately. Anaphylaxis can stop the heart. Continue until paramedics arrive.

Biphasic reactions: Up to 20% of anaphylaxis cases have a second wave of symptoms 1–72 hours after the first. Even if the person feels completely better, they must be taken to hospital for observation. Never let them go home alone after anaphylaxis.

Poisoning — Ingestion, Inhalation & Contact

The correct response differs by exposure route — don't delay in seeking help

Watch Practical Demonstration
Arabic

Call 911 or Poison Control Immediately

Have the substance name or container to hand. Don't wait for symptoms to appear. The earlier you call, the better the outcome.

If Swallowed

Do not induce vomiting unless explicitly told to by Poison Control. Keep the person calm and upright. Preserve the container — the label helps paramedics.

If Inhaled

Move them to fresh air immediately. Open windows. Never enter a gas-filled space without breathing protection — you will become a casualty too.

If on Skin or in Eyes

Skin: remove contaminated clothing and flush with plenty of water for 20 minutes. Eyes: irrigate with clean water or saline for at least 15 minutes — hold the eyelids open and flush from the inner corner outward.

Monitor Closely

Watch for any change in consciousness, breathing, or skin colour. If unconscious but breathing, use the recovery position. If they stop breathing — begin CPR.

Never give anything by mouth to an unconscious or seizing person. Do not give milk, activated charcoal, or any home remedies without direct instruction from Poison Control. Some substances react violently with certain antidotes.

Medical Shock — Recognise & Act

A life-threatening condition where organs don't receive enough blood flow

Recognise the Signs of Shock

Pale, cold, clammy skin; rapid and weak pulse; fast, shallow breathing; dizziness and confusion; nausea or intense thirst. Skin may turn grey or have a blue tinge around the lips.

Call 911 Immediately

Shock is always a medical emergency. Call while beginning first aid — do not delay emergency services.

Lay Them Down Gently

Help them to the ground carefully. Avoid unnecessary movement. Keep them as still as possible.

Raise Their Legs

Unless there is a suspected head, neck, or leg fracture, raise the legs approximately 30 cm to improve blood flow to vital organs. Do not raise the legs if breathing becomes harder.

Keep Warm & Treat the Cause

Cover with a blanket (avoid overheating). Control any bleeding. Loosen tight clothing around the neck, chest, and waist.

Reassure & Monitor Continuously

Talk to them calmly. Give nothing by mouth. Check breathing and pulse every few minutes until paramedics arrive.

Anaphylactic shock specifically requires adrenaline (EpiPen) — see the Anaphylaxis guide. Septic shock requires urgent IV antibiotics at hospital. Shock from bleeding requires controlling the bleed first — see the Severe Bleeding guide.

Diabetic Crisis — Low & High Blood Sugar

Recognise whether blood sugar is dangerously low (hypo) or high (hyper) — the responses differ

Watch Practical Demonstration
English

Identify the Type of Crisis

Hypoglycaemia (low sugar): sudden dizziness, shaking, sweating, pale skin, confusion, hunger, irritability — acts quickly. Hyperglycaemia (high sugar): gradual onset, fruity breath, extreme thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, nausea.

Hypoglycaemia — Give Sugar Immediately

If the person is conscious: give 15–20g of fast-acting sugar — a glass of fruit juice, sugary drink, glucose tablets, or 3–4 teaspoons of sugar. Wait 15 minutes. If no improvement, give more. Once recovered, give a longer-acting snack (crackers, sandwich).

Hyperglycaemia — Water & Medical Help

Encourage the person to drink water and contact their doctor or call 111. If they are unconscious, very unwell, or vomiting, call 911. Do not give insulin unless you are a trained carer for this person.

Call 911 If Unconscious

Never give anything by mouth to an unconscious person. If they lose consciousness, call 911 immediately, place them in the recovery position, and begin CPR if they stop breathing.

Hypo Rule of 15

Give 15g sugar → wait 15 minutes → check again → if still low, repeat once more → then a substantial snack. If no improvement after 2 rounds, seek emergency help.

Key distinction: If you are unsure whether blood sugar is low or high, and the person is conscious, giving sugar is safer — a little extra sugar in someone with high blood sugar does much less harm than low sugar going untreated. When in doubt, give sugar.

Seizures — What to Do During & After

Protect, time, and observe — most seizures end on their own within 1–3 minutes

Watch Practical Demonstration
English

Time the Seizure

Note when it starts. Most seizures end within 1–3 minutes. A seizure lasting over 5 minutes is a medical emergency — call 911.

Protect From Injury

Clear hard or sharp objects away from the person. Cushion the head with something soft. Gently guide them away from danger but do not restrain their movements.

What NOT to Do

Never put anything in their mouth — the tongue cannot be swallowed. Never restrain them. Never give water until fully conscious. Never leave them alone during the seizure.

After the Seizure — Recovery Position

When convulsions stop, gently roll them onto their side (recovery position) to keep the airway clear. They may be confused or sleepy — this is normal (postictal state) and can last minutes to hours.

Call 911 If Any of These Apply

The seizure lasts over 5 minutes; another seizure starts shortly after; they don't regain consciousness; they are injured; they are pregnant; they are diabetic; or it is their first known seizure.

After the seizure: Speak calmly and reassuringly. They may be disoriented and frightened. Explain what happened in simple terms. Stay with them until they are fully alert and oriented. Inform them they should see their doctor regardless, especially for a first seizure.

Golden Rules

Do & Don't in First Aid

Common mistakes make injuries worse — know the rules before you need them

Always Do
  • Call emergency services first in serious situations
  • Stay calm — your composure reassures the casualty
  • Use gloves or a barrier between you and blood
  • Keep the person still unless in immediate danger
  • Note the time of injury and any medications taken
  • Reassure them continuously — psychological support matters
  • Follow dispatcher instructions precisely and calmly
  • Place unconscious but breathing persons in recovery position
Never Do
  • Don't remove an object embedded in a wound
  • Don't apply ice directly to skin or burns
  • Don't move someone with a suspected spinal injury
  • Don't give food or drink to an unconscious person
  • Don't induce vomiting after poisoning without medical advice
  • Don't remove a crash helmet without clear need
  • Don't leave them alone — stay until help arrives
  • Don't apply butter, toothpaste, or oil to burns
